\section{System and Subsystem Requirements}
\label{sec:requirements}

\subsection{System Engineering Process}

The \teamname{} (AVL) at \university{} presents \robotname{}, our entry for the IGVC
2026 AutoNav challenge. AVL is an undergraduate research lab within the College of
Engineering focused on autonomy, embedded systems, and ground-vehicle perception. The
IGVC subteam of \placeholder{N} students is organized across software, mechanical, and
electrical disciplines, advised by Dr.~Behnam Bahr, and led by Parsa Ghasemi as project
captain. \robotname{} is a fully autonomous differential-drive tracked vehicle built on
the AndyMark Raptor chassis, fusing a Velodyne VLP-16 3D LiDAR, three Stereolabs ZED~X
stereo cameras, and an Xsens MTi-680G IMU+GNSS, with autonomy running on an NVIDIA
Jetson Orin under ROS~2 Humble. The team followed an iterative \textit{research
$\rightarrow$ design $\rightarrow$ prototype $\rightarrow$ test} cycle: rulebook
analysis $\to$ system-level requirements; parallel subsystem development; incremental
bench/actuator/fusion/Nav2 integration; and iterative field testing at the
Cal Poly Pomona Engineering Meadow to tune HSV thresholds, EKF covariances, costmap
parameters, and MPPI critic weights. Figure~\ref{fig:system_arch} shows the resulting
system architecture.

\section{Mechanical Design}
\label{sec:mechanical}

\subsection{Overview}

As first-year IGVC participants, the mechanical subteam prioritized a simple, modular,
and cost-effective platform that concentrates budget on the sensors and compute
required for autonomous navigation. \robotname{} is built on the commercially available
AndyMark\textsuperscript{\textregistered} Raptor Track Drive, a tracked differential
base selected for its all-terrain traction and short delivery lead time. A reconfigurable
80/20 T-slot aluminum superstructure is mounted directly to the chassis, allowing rapid
iteration on sensor mounts and shelf geometry through the design cycle. The same chassis
can host different electronics housings for future competitions; for IGVC~2026 the
housing is mounted on the upper shelf so that the electronics, payload, and E-stop are
all readily accessible during operation and inspection. The integrated vehicle is shown
on the cover page; a rear-three-quarter view is in Figure~\ref{fig:cad_rear}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth, trim=10 10 10 10, clip]{drafts/mechanical/figures/cad_rear.png}
    \caption{Rear three-quarter view of \robotname{}; the rear shelf supports the mechanical E-stop and extends the chassis to meet the IGVC 3~ft length minimum.}
    \label{fig:cad_rear}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Drivetrain}

\robotname{} uses a tracked \textbf{differential-drive} architecture: two REV NEO
brushless motors, each commanded by a REV SparkMAX controller over CAN, independently
drive the left and right track loops. The choice eliminates steering linkages, reduces
the part count, and produces a control plant that maps cleanly onto the ROS~2 Nav2 / MPPI
controller running on the Jetson. With $v_L$ and $v_R$ the left and right track linear
velocities, the body-frame linear and angular rates are
\begin{align}
    v &= \tfrac{1}{2}(v_R + v_L), &
    \omega &= \tfrac{1}{b}(v_R - v_L),
\end{align}
where $b$ is the track width (effective lever arm between contact patches). The planner
uses three operating modes: pivot turn (one track held, robot rotates about the
stationary contact patch); in-place spin ($v_R = -v_L$, ICR at the vehicle center,
maximum yaw rate); and gradual curve (both tracks forward, $v_R \neq v_L$).

The full electromechanical signal path (also shown in Figure~\ref{fig:system_arch} and
Figure~\ref{fig:electrical-block}) is: Jetson Orin issues
\texttt{geometry\_msgs/Twist} at 50~Hz over USB serial to the Teensy~4.1; the Teensy
decomposes commands into per-side wheel velocities and forwards them over CAN at
1~Mbit/s to the two SparkMAX controllers; each SparkMAX closes a 1~kHz velocity loop
around its NEO motor using the integrated Hall encoders.

\subsection{Frame, Housing, and Sensor Mast}

The Raptor base contributes the drive sprockets, idlers, and rubber-track belts that
distribute vehicle weight over a large contact patch, improving traction and stability
on the uneven turf and gravel sections of the IGVC course. A $21.5'' \times 30''$,
$1/8''$ thick A36 diamond-plate steel base is bolted directly to the Raptor mounting
rails. The diamond plate serves three functions: (i) a rigid surface for heavy
components, (ii) an EMI-attenuating ground plane between battery and tracked drive, and
(iii) a low-mounted mass that lowers the center of gravity. The superstructure is built
from 80/20\textsuperscript{\textregistered} 20-Series T-slot aluminum extrusion --- modular,
reconfigurable, and free of exposed threaded ends.

Weight placement was an explicit design priority. The 48~V battery is mounted at the
geometric center of the diamond plate; the 20~lb payload bay sits at the front of the
deck on a 3D-printed cradle. The resulting center of gravity sits slightly forward of
the track midpoint and low on the deck, minimizing pitch moment on inclines and during
skid-steer turns. The diamond-plate deck is $21.5'' \times 30''$ and the upper sensor
shelf is $23'' \times 23'' \times 6''$ mounted 20~in above ground; the rear shelf
carries the E-stop button at $24''$ (Figure~\ref{fig:cad_rear}). The chassis was
extended with that rear shelf to satisfy both the IGVC 3~ft minimum length and the
2--4~ft E-stop mounting requirement.

\subsection{Weatherproofing}

Outdoor operation requires the electronics bay to tolerate rain and direct sun. The
sensor-shelf walls are cut-to-fit acrylic panels that slide directly into the 20-Series
T-slot grooves; weatherstripping gasket fills the residual gap and remains seated under
track vibration. The top panel is mildly angled for water runoff and incorporates a
sliding access door. The LiDAR is mounted externally above the shelf to keep its
$360^\circ$ field of view unobstructed; the ZED~X cameras sit behind AR-coated windows.

\section{Safety}
\label{sec:safety}

\subsection{Transport, Parked, and Charging Safety}

\textbf{Transport.} MARVIN is moved between the lab and the Cal Poly Pomona Engineering
Meadow under manual control via the AVROS web UI joystick over Wi-Fi; the software
E-stop in the actuator node is armed throughout transit, and autonomous mode is never
enabled outside the test area. For the long-haul trip to the IGVC competition venue at
Oakland University, MARVIN is loaded into a cargo van and secured with ratchet straps
anchored to the 80/20 frame and diamond-plate deck so it cannot shift, tip, or roll
during the drive.

\textbf{Parked.} The mechanical E-stop is latched (pushed in) by default whenever
MARVIN is not actively running, both between practice runs and during long-term storage;
it is only released when the team is preparing to test movement. The main power switch
is set OFF when parked overnight. The acrylic panels and weatherstripping protect the
electronics bay from rain and dust during outdoor parking.

\textbf{Bench testing.} For drivetrain checks (track tension, gearbox inspection, motor
characterization, encoder calibration), MARVIN is raised onto a workstation so the
tracks turn freely without translating the vehicle. This eliminates the risk of
uncommanded motion during firmware tuning and lets the team probe live signals safely
with the vehicle de-coupled from the floor.

\textbf{Charging.} The 48~V 25~Ah Aegis LiFePO\textsubscript{4} pack remains installed
in MARVIN during charging; the DC House 48~V 18~A charger connects to a dedicated,
keyed charge port wired into the vehicle's power system, so reverse-polarity and
wrong-connector incidents are mechanically impossible. The charger is rated IP65 with
built-in overheat, overcurrent, overvoltage, reverse-polarity, and short-circuit
protections, and LiFePO\textsubscript{4} chemistry is intrinsically thermally stable
and non-volatile compared to NMC lithium --- eliminating the runaway-fire risk
associated with most lithium-ion packs. All charging is performed in the lab (not at
the competition venue, where the team operates exclusively on pre-charged batteries)
with a fire extinguisher within reach; because of the chemistry and charger
protections, charges are permitted to run unattended.

\subsection{On-Course Safety}

The on-course safety architecture (Figure~\ref{fig:safety_block}) implements a
hardware-enforced two-channel emergency stop: either the rear-mounted mechanical E-stop
button or the wireless remote E-stop receiver opens a relay in series with the
motor-controller enable line. When the relay opens, the SparkMAX controllers immediately
disable PWM output and brake the NEO motors via the integrated short-brake mode;
software is not in the loop, so a Jetson hang or ROS crash cannot defeat the stop.
Indicator LEDs visible from outside the chassis show the armed/disarmed state for the
IGVC safety inspection.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \input{drafts/mechanical/figures/safety_block.tex}
    \caption{Two-channel emergency stop. The mechanical (rear-shelf) and wireless E-stops
    are wired in series with the motor-enable relay; either one open de-energizes both
    SparkMAX controllers, independent of software state.}
    \label{fig:safety_block}
\end{figure}

In software, the actuator node additionally zeroes \texttt{cmd\_vel} on stale input
(300~ms watchdog) so a Jetson stall or ROS-node crash defaults the vehicle to a stop
even before the hardware relay reacts. A firmware speed clamp at $\pm$4600~RPM
(1.53~m/s ground speed) further limits the worst-case motion even if upstream commands
are malformed.

\subsection{Overview}

The 2026 electrical architecture is organised into four subsystems: power
distribution, network and computation, sensing, and motor control.
Figure~\ref{fig:electrical-block} shows how these subsystems interconnect.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Power} --- a 48~V battery feeds two buck converters (12~V and 5~V

    (\S\ref{sec:electrical}.5).
  \item \textbf{Sensing} --- three ZED~X stereo cameras, a Velodyne LiDAR, and an
    Xsens GPS feed the Jetson (\S\ref{sec:electrical}.4).
  \item \textbf{Motor control} --- the Teensy runs a closed-loop controller and
    commands two SPARK~MAX motor controllers over CAN, which in turn drive
    two NEO brushless motors (\S\ref{sec:electrical}.3).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Power Distribution}

The vehicle is powered by an Aegis 48~V 25~Ah LiFePO\textsubscript{4} deep-cycle
lithium battery (1.2~kWh nominal). LiFePO\textsubscript{4} chemistry was selected for
its thermal stability, $\sim$2000-cycle service life, and tolerance to partial-state-of-charge
operation between practice runs. The battery passes through a main switch into two
buck converters (also shown in Figure~\ref{fig:electrical-block}): a 12~V / 20~A
converter for the motor controllers and other high-power loads, and a 5~V / 6~A
converter for the Teensy~4.1 and low-power peripherals. Each rail is split into three
independently fused output branches (battery $\to$ switch $\to$ buck $\to$ 3 fused
branches per rail) so a short on one load does not pull down the rest of the vehicle.

\textbf{Run time and recharge.} A 25~Ah pack at 48~V stores $\sim$1200~Wh. Estimated
nominal load under autonomous operation is $\sim$155~W (Jetson Orin $\sim$30~W,
VLP-16 LiDAR 8~W, 3$\times$ ZED~X $\sim$9~W, Xsens IMU/GPS 1~W, router 5~W, Teensy
$<$1~W, drive motors $\sim$100~W average over typical IGVC speeds), which corresponds
to $\sim$3.2~A at the 48~V bus. Allowing for buck-converter and battery losses
($\eta_{\text{tot}} \approx 0.85$), the calculated runtime is
$\sim$1200~Wh~$\times$~0.85~/~155~W~$\approx$~6.5~hours --- two orders of magnitude
above the 6-minute IGVC II.2 course ceiling and enough for a full practice day on a
single charge under realistic load.

The pack is recharged with a DC House 48~V 18~A LiFePO\textsubscript{4}
portable charger (model S-US-L131211800092-1, 58.4~V CV output, 18~A bulk-charge
current, IP65). At 18~A bulk current, a fully depleted 25~Ah pack reaches $\sim$80\%
state of charge in $\sim$1.4~hours; with the LiFePO\textsubscript{4} absorption phase
and cell balancing, a full recharge takes approximately 1.5--2~hours. Charging is
performed outside the vehicle with the battery disconnected from the drive bus.

\subsection{Motor Control}

The drivetrain uses two NEO brushless motors, each driven by a SPARK~MAX motor
controller. Both controllers share a single CAN bus to the Teensy~4.1, which acts as the
real-time control node. Each SPARK~MAX reports position and velocity from its integrated
encoder back over the same bus. The Teensy closes a PID velocity loop per wheel, using
encoder feedback from the SPARK~MAX controllers and reference velocities published by
the Jetson over USB serial. Because the motor controllers, the encoders, and the
microcontroller all live on one CAN bus, no separate signal wiring between the motor
controllers and the Teensy is required.

\textbf{Firmware.} The Teensy 4.1 translates ASCII RPM commands into SparkMAX CAN
velocity setpoints at 1~Mbit/s and echoes encoder feedback at 50~Hz. Three independent
timeouts protect the drivetrain: a 300~ms serial watchdog on the Teensy, a 100~ms CAN
heartbeat timeout per SparkMAX, and a firmware clamp of $\pm$4600~RPM (1.53~m/s ground
speed, below the 5~mph IGVC cap).

\subsection{Sensors}

\textbf{Stereo cameras.} Three ZED~X cameras (front-left, front, front-right) connect
to a ZED~Link Quad over GMSL2; the ZED~Link Quad attaches to the Jetson over PCIe,
keeping the cameras off the Ethernet network.

\textbf{LiDAR.} A Velodyne VLP-16 provides 360$^\circ$ 3D point clouds at 10~Hz. It
reports to the Jetson over Ethernet through the on-board router.

\textbf{GPS / IMU.} An Xsens MTi-680G provides 9-axis inertial data at 100~Hz and a
standalone GNSS fix at 5~Hz (RTK disabled per IGVC rules). It connects directly to the
Jetson over USB.

\subsection{Network and Computation}

The Jetson Orin is the primary computation unit; it runs perception, sensor fusion, and
trajectory generation, and publishes wheel velocity commands to the Teensy. An on-board
router carries Ethernet between the Jetson and the Velodyne LiDAR. The Teensy~4.1
connects to the Jetson via USB serial (115200 baud) rather than Ethernet. The
high-bandwidth camera link is kept separate: the ZED~Link Quad attaches directly to the
Jetson over PCIe so that the three ZED~X feeds do not contend for the Ethernet network.
The Xsens GPS connects directly to the Jetson. The same router also exposes a Wi-Fi
link to a remote laptop for monitoring, data capture, and remote shutdown during
testing.

\section{Perception}
\label{sec:perception}

The perception stack splits responsibilities between LiDAR (physical obstacles) and
cameras (painted lane lines and color-coded objects), with both feeding a unified
costmap consumed by the driving-logic layer. We are targeting the AutoNav challenge, so
this section discusses lane and obstacle perception specific to the AutoNav course.

\subsection{LiDAR Perception (Velodyne VLP-16)}

The VLP-16 publishes a 360$^\circ$ point cloud at 10~Hz into Nav2's
\texttt{spatio\_temporal\_voxel\_layer} (STVL), which maintains a 0.1~m voxel grid with
5~s linear voxel decay. A minimum obstacle height of 0.2~m filters ground hits and low
grass while keeping short barrels and pedestrians. Detection range is 1--15~m local and
1--50~m global. STVL replaces the standard \texttt{VoxelLayer} because the VLP-16's
16-beam $\pm$15$^\circ$ vertical FOV leaves angular gaps that defeat ray-trace clearing
when the robot is stationary; STVL clears via time-based voxel decay plus a
frustum-accelerated decay term that handles the sparse-scan case cleanly.

\subsection{Vision Perception (3$\times$ ZED~X)}

Each ZED~X publishes rectified HD1080 color and a COMPACT-resolution organized point
cloud at 10~Hz. The \texttt{avros\_perception} package runs one \texttt{perception\_node}
per camera; each subscribes to its camera's RGB and cloud topics through an
\texttt{ApproximateTimeSynchronizer} (20~ms slop) and runs a pluggable pipeline. The
production pipeline (\texttt{hsv}) performs three-class HSV thresholding (white lane,
orange barrel, pothole) with an adaptive value-channel floor
($V_{\text{floor}} = \mu_V + k\sigma_V$, recomputed every $N$ frames) and a polygonal
sky-rejection ROI. Class IDs: 1 = lane, 2 = barrel, 3 = pothole. All thresholds are
runtime-tunable via \texttt{ros2 param set} for field calibration.

Every pipeline outputs the four-topic contract consumed by the
\texttt{kiwicampus/semantic\_segmentation\_layer}: a mono8 class-ID mask, a mono8
confidence plane, the organized point cloud (relayed), and a latched
\texttt{vision\_msgs/LabelInfo}.

\subsection{Internal Course Representation (Costmap)}

Perception outputs are fused into two Nav2 costmaps: a 50$\times$50~m rolling local
costmap at 0.2~m resolution (\texttt{stvl\_layer} + 3$\times$ \texttt{semantic\_layer}
front/left/right + \texttt{inflation\_layer}) and a 100$\times$100~m rolling global
costmap (\texttt{obstacle\_layer} + \texttt{inflation\_layer}). The robot footprint is
rectangular $1.00 \times 0.74$~m with $r_{\text{inscr}} = 0.5$~m and an inflation radius
of 0.65~m. STVL voxel decay (5~s) keeps stale cells from blocking the planner after the
robot moves; semantic raytrace clearing handles cells that have been re-observed as
free.

\subsection{AutoNav Course-Specific Behavior}

The AutoNav course (IGVC II.2) features painted white lane lines $\sim 3''$ wide on
asphalt, randomly placed construction barrels (white/orange/brown/green/black) at
$\geq 5$~ft clearance from the lane, an $\leq 15\%$ gradient ramp, simulated potholes
(2~ft white circles), and GPS waypoint pairs at course entry/exit and ramp entry. The
HSV pipeline tagged with class~1 isolates white lane paint; barrels register under
class~2; potholes register under class~3; the VLP-16 STVL marks any other physical
obstacle. A startup auto-calibration routine samples the ground at run start and adapts
the $V$-channel threshold to current lighting before mobility is enabled.

\section{Driving Logic}
\label{sec:drivinglogic}

The driving-logic stack converts the perception costmap and current pose into wheel
commands. Nav2 provides path planning, trajectory generation, and behavior orchestration
(\S\ref{sec:drivinglogic}.1); \texttt{robot\_localization} maintains the pose
(\S\ref{sec:drivinglogic}.2); and the actuator node converts \texttt{cmd\_vel} to wheel
RPM (\S\ref{sec:drivinglogic}.3). AutoNav-specific course handling --- lane following,
obstacle avoidance, ramp, and waypoint navigation --- is described in
\S\ref{sec:drivinglogic}.4.

\subsection{Planning and Path Generation}

MPPI samples 2{,}000 rollout trajectories per 20~Hz control cycle, scores them against
eight plugin-based critics (constraint, cost, goal, goal-angle, path-align, path-follow,
path-angle, prefer-forward), and emits the noise-weighted optimal command. Unlike
pursuit controllers that only \textit{follow} a path, MPPI \textit{generates} trajectories
that deviate from the global plan to avoid newly observed obstacles --- critical for
IGVC's randomly-placed barrels.

\textbf{Behavior tree.} The active BT (\texttt{navigate\_igvc\_autonav\_humble.xml})
wraps the standard plan-follow pipeline with several IGVC-specific guards. An outer
\texttt{Timeout} of 45~s margins the 60~s hold-up-traffic disqualification rule. A
\texttt{RecoveryNode} retries the pipeline up to three times; recovery actions form a
\texttt{RoundRobin} escalating clear-costmap $\to$ wait $\to$ short backup $\to$ crawl
forward. Spin-in-place is intentionally omitted: the $1.00 \times 0.74$~m footprint
sweeps a 2.48~m diameter circle when off-center, exceeding the half-lane width.

\textbf{Waypoint orchestration.} The \texttt{mission\_manager} node loads competition
waypoints from \texttt{waypoints.yaml} as a lat/lon list, converts each to map frame via
\texttt{/fromLL}, and fires \texttt{NavigateToPose} action goals sequentially. The
cursor advances on a 2~m acceptance radius (IGVC rule); \texttt{ABORTED}/\texttt{CANCELED}
failures fall through to the next waypoint.

\subsection{Localization}

Localization uses two instances of \texttt{robot\_localization}'s EKF; both run at
30~Hz in 2D mode. The local EKF (\texttt{odom} $\to$ \texttt{base\_link}) fuses Xsens
IMU (100~Hz), wheel odometry from the actuator node (50~Hz), and ZED~X VIO yaw
(yaw-only, fused differentially to avoid the lever-arm bias from the camera's offset
mount). The global EKF (\texttt{map} $\to$ \texttt{odom}) fuses the IMU stream with GPS
odometry from \texttt{navsat\_transform\_node} in differential mode with a 6$\sigma$
Mahalanobis outlier-rejection threshold. Integrating per-step GPS deltas rather than
absolute fixes prevents costmap jumps from $\pm 2$--5~m consumer-grade GNSS noise (RTK
is disabled per IGVC rules).

\subsection{Drive Control}
\label{sec:control}

The \texttt{actuator\_node} bridges Nav2 to the physical drivetrain. It subscribes to
\texttt{/cmd\_vel} (from Nav2, teleop, or the velocity smoother) and runs a 50~Hz loop:
(1) source arbitration; (2) slew-rate limit (linear 0.5~m/s$^2$ accel, 1.5~m/s$^2$
brake; angular 1.0~rad/s$^2$); (3) IMU-based heading hold ($K_p=1.5$) when
$|\omega_{\text{cmd}}|<0.05$~rad/s, or gyro-stabilized turn ($K_p=0.3$) otherwise;
(4) diff-drive inverse kinematics with \texttt{track\_width\_m=0.7366}, \texttt{m\_per\_motor\_rev=0.01994};
(5) ASCII serial write to the Teensy at 115200~baud. In parallel, encoder feedback is
integrated into a 50~Hz \texttt{/wheel\_odom} message that the local EKF consumes.

\subsection{AutoNav Driving Logic}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lane following:} HSV white-lane masks feed the local costmap's
    \texttt{semantic\_layer} as lethal cells; MPPI generates rollouts that stay inside
    the lane corridor.
    \item \textbf{Obstacle avoidance:} VLP-16 STVL marks barrels in both costmaps; MPPI's
    \texttt{PreferForwardCritic} and \texttt{PathFollowCritic} bias toward forward motion
    along the global plan when no obstacles intrude.
    \item \textbf{Ramp handling:} The 2D-mode EKF assumption holds for the IGVC ramp;
    pitch is monitored from the IMU. MPPI's cost-regulated velocity scaling
    automatically slows the robot near costmap inflation.
    \item \textbf{Switchbacks and dead-ends:} The BT recovery escalation
    (clear-costmap $\to$ wait 2~s $\to$ backup 1.5~m at 0.08~m/s $\to$ crawl forward
    0.15~m at 0.10~m/s) reorients the planner when the global plan is repeatedly
    blocked, allowing the robot to back out of dead-ends and switchbacks.
    \item \textbf{Potholes:} The HSV pipeline's class~3 channel marks the IGVC 2~ft
    white-circle simulated potholes as lethal in the local costmap; MPPI rollouts that
    would enter a pothole are scored as collisions and rejected.
    \item \textbf{Traps and complex obstacles:} The 50$\times$50~m local costmap holds
    obstacles long enough for MPPI to consider alternate paths; the inflation gradient
    ($k_{\text{scale}}=3.0$) gives MPPI a smooth cost field so rollouts naturally
    funnel through the widest gap between barrels.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Target rationale.} Course completion is set at $\leq 5$~min, one minute under
the IGVC II.2 six-minute ceiling, leaving margin for a slow restart after a
\texttt{RecoveryNode} fallback. Maximum speed is hardware-governed at 3.4~mph
(below the 5~mph rule cap and above the 1~mph minimum hold-up threshold), giving the
MPPI controller a stable operating point. Battery runtime targets at least one full
6~min course run plus margin for warm-up and a queued retry. The wireless E-stop has
been verified to operate beyond the IGVC 100~ft minimum with a comfortable margin to
absorb multipath and antenna-orientation variability during the open-field range test.

\subsection{Safety, Reliability, and Durability}

Safety is enforced at every layer: a hardware speed cap (4600~RPM $\to$ 1.53~m/s) in the
Teensy firmware, a 300~ms serial watchdog on the Teensy, a 100~ms CAN heartbeat timeout
per SparkMAX, a software slew-rate limit in the actuator node, and Nav2's MPPI collision
critic plus a costmap inflation radius of 0.65~m. The MPPI \texttt{CostCritic} uses
\texttt{consider\_footprint: true} so the rectangular $1.00 \times 0.74$~m footprint is
swept along every rollout trajectory.

EKF covariances, Nav2 gains, MPPI critic weights, actuator slew limits --- live in
version-controlled YAML.

\section{Cyber Security Analysis}
\label{sec:cyber}

Modern robotic and human-driven vehicles are network-connected, contain large amounts
of software from multiple vendors, and are an inviting target for cyber-attacks. Per
IGVC 2026 rule IV.3 \S11, we list three cyber vulnerabilities present in our vehicle
and discuss the steps that should be taken to harden or remove them before the system
enters series production.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \footnotesize
    \caption{Top three cyber vulnerabilities and hardening steps.}
    \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{l X X}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Vulnerability} & \textbf{Risk} & \textbf{Hardening / Remediation} \\
        \midrule
        Onboard network breach
        & Wi-Fi foothold exposes the ROS~2 discovery service (unauthenticated by default), allowing \texttt{cmd\_vel} injection.
        & Dedicated subnet with WPA2 (SC-7); CycloneDDS Security with X.509 identities and access-control governance (AC-3). \\
        \addlinespace
        WebSocket joystick hijack
        & The \texttt{avros\_webui} WebSocket can override autonomous \texttt{cmd\_vel} from a local-network attacker.
        & WSS with pinned cert; session token at pair time; fail-safe disconnect $\to$ E-stop (AC-3, IA-2). \\
        \addlinespace
        GPS spoofing / multipath
        & Spoofed GPS could feed the global EKF a false position, advancing the waypoint cursor prematurely.
        & Differential GPS fusion (per-step deltas); 6$\sigma$ Mahalanobis outlier rejection (SI-4, SI-7); cross-check GPS heading vs IMU heading. \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

In addition to the three vulnerabilities above, safety-critical motion is gated by
three independent stop mechanisms (physical E-stop, wireless E-stop, software interlock
in the actuator node) so a single cyber-compromise of the autonomy stack does not by
itself produce uncontrolled motion --- the hardware relay must be re-enabled by a human
operator.
